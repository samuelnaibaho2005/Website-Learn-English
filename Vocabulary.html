What is vocabulary?

Vocabulary refers to the set of words known and used by a person in a language. It encompasses all the words that a person understands and can employ in communication, both in spoken and written forms. Vocabulary can be divided into active and passive categories. Active vocabulary consists of words that a person regularly uses in speaking and writing. These words are well understood and easily accessible during communication. Passive vocabulary includes words that a person recognizes and understands when encountered in reading or listening but does not use frequently in active communication.

Vocabulary is composed of different components. The basic units are words, which include nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and other parts of speech. Phrases are groups of words that convey specific meanings when used together, such as idioms or collocations. Expressions are common sayings or idiomatic phrases that carry specific meanings within a language.

A robust vocabulary is crucial for effective communication, allowing individuals to express their ideas clearly and accurately. It is also essential for comprehension, as understanding vocabulary is key to interpreting and making sense of content in reading and listening. In language learning, expanding vocabulary is fundamental as it enriches one's ability to engage with the language in various contexts.

Types of Vocabulary

In general, vocabulary is divided into two categories, namely active vocabulary and passive vocabulary.

  1. Active Vocabulary
    2.

    Active vocabulary is a collection of words that are understood and used when speaking and writing in everyday life.

    1. Eat: "I eat breakfast every morning."
    2. Run: "She runs five kilometers every day."
    3. Talk: "They talk about their plans for the weekend."
    4. Read: "He reads a book before going to bed."
    5. Write: "I write emails to my colleagues."
  2. Passive Vocabulary
    3.

    Passive vocabulary are words that we may recognize, but are not commonly used in normal communication.
    Example:

    1. Astonished: "She was astonished by the unexpected news."
    2. Exquisite: "The painting was truly exquisite."
    3. Perplexed: "He looked perplexed by the complex problem."
    4. Magnanimous: "Her magnanimous gesture was appreciated by all."
    5. Obsolete: "That technology is now considered obsolete."
  3. Expressive Vocabulary
    4.

    Expressive vocabulary is the vocabulary we use to express our thoughts, namely the words we use in speaking and writing.
    Example:

    1. Frustrated: "I feel frustrated when things don't go as planned."
    2. Elated: "She was elated when she heard the good news."
    3. Desperate: "He was desperate for a solution to the problem."
    4. Curious: "I'm curious about how this works."
    5. Optimistic: "Despite the challenges, he remained optimistic."
  4. Receptive Vocabulary
    5.

    Receptive vocabulary is the vocabulary that we understand in reading and listening, or all the vocabulary that we process when receiving information.
    Example:

    1. Analyze: "The scientist will analyze the data carefully."
    2. Contextualize: "It's important to contextualize historical events."
    3. Hypothesis: "The researcher proposed a new hypothesis."
    4. Synthesize: "Students are required to synthesize information from multiple sources."
    5. Evaluate: "We need to evaluate the effectiveness of the new policy."
